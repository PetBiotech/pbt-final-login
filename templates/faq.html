The use of PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction) in molecular testing has become the industry standard for diagnosing diseases. Diagnostic, research, and monitoring opportunities made possible by PCR's sensitivity, specificity, and speed in identifying infections and mutations are not possible with other procedures. The diagnosis of sickness in dogs, cats, and other animals can be greatly aided by molecular approaches. In PCR testing, nucleic acid (DNA or RNA) is extracted from a specimen and amplified using highly specific primers complementary to known target sequences (with an additional reverse transcription step in the case of RNA). Internal controls track extraction and PCR effectiveness while both positive and negative controls are active at the same time. To check for repeatability, each sample is run in duplicate aliquots. Modern protocols and sophisticated instruments are used to visualize and record amplification.

  • Cross-reactivity decreases the specificity of many serological tests since it is possible for organisms other than the target to produce a positive result. On the other hand, because they identify a genetic sequence that is particular to the target pathogen, molecular assays are extremely specific. The ability to differentiate even closely related disease strains is common.
  • While other testing techniques detect antibodies to infections that may not still be present, molecular assays detect the genetic material of the pathogen itself — a clear indication of the pathogen's presence in the sample — which reduces the influence of some kinds of false positives.
  • By using PCR, infections are frequently detected relatively early in the onset of symptoms. Elevated antibody levels, which may not appear for days or weeks, are not necessary for detection.
  • Animals with high viral titers may actually show relatively low antibody levels as an infection stresses their immune systems. If antibody testing is the only method employed, this may lead to erroneous negative findings.
  • Because of its exceptional sensitivity, PCR can detect a target pathogen in a sample, even when it is present in extremely minute quantities and from very small sample volumes.

Fill EDTA (purple top) tubes with freshly drawn whole blood samples, seal the tubes, and then quickly invert the tubes several times to fully mix the blood with the preservation material. Though we can test from heparinized blood, avoiding using heparin (green top) tubes is preferable. Other liquid samples should be collected in simple tubes or other clean, leak-proof containers, such as lavage, serum, or cerebrospinal fluid. If shipping won't be completed for more than a few hours following collection, refrigerate liquid samples. Unless there is a likelihood that samples will be in transit for longer than 24 hours, room temperature shipment is sufficient.
